Pharmacy technicians play a critical role in the healthcare system, working alongside pharmacists to ensure patients receive the right medications safely and efficiently. With growing demand for healthcare services and expanding roles for pharmacy technicians, this career path offers stability, decent pay, and multiple opportunities for advancement.

If you are considering becoming a certified pharmacy technician in 2026, this guide covers everything from education requirements to exam preparation to long-term career prospects.

## What Does a Pharmacy Technician Do?

Pharmacy technicians assist pharmacists with the day-to-day operations of a pharmacy. The specific duties vary depending on whether you work in a retail pharmacy, hospital, mail-order pharmacy, or specialty pharmacy, but common responsibilities include:

- Receiving and processing prescription orders
- Counting, measuring, and packaging medications
- Managing inventory and ordering supplies
- Processing insurance claims and billing
- Answering phone calls and assisting customers
- Maintaining patient records in pharmacy software systems
- Preparing sterile compounded medications (in hospital and specialty settings)

It is important to understand that pharmacy technicians work under the supervision of a licensed pharmacist. Technicians cannot independently counsel patients on medications, verify prescriptions, or make clinical decisions. However, the scope of technician responsibilities has been expanding steadily, with many states now allowing technicians to perform tasks that were previously reserved for pharmacists.

## Education and Training Requirements

### Formal Education Programs

While some states allow pharmacy technicians to learn on the job, completing a formal education program significantly improves your employment prospects and prepares you for certification exams.

**Certificate programs** are the most common pathway. These programs typically last 6 to 12 months and are offered by community colleges, vocational schools, and some pharmacy chains. They cover pharmaceutical calculations, pharmacology basics, pharmacy law, medication safety, and hands-on dispensing practice.

**Associate degree programs** take about two years and provide a more comprehensive education, including general education courses alongside pharmacy-specific training. An associate degree can open doors to supervisory positions and may be preferred by hospital pharmacies.

### On-the-Job Training

Some employers, particularly large retail chains like CVS, Walgreens, and Walmart, offer their own training programs for pharmacy technicians. These programs allow you to earn while you learn, but they typically require you to obtain certification within a specified timeframe after completing training.

### ASHP/ACPE Accreditation

When choosing a program, look for accreditation by the American Society of Health-System Pharmacists (ASHP) in partnership with the Accreditation Council for Pharmacy Education (ACPE). Accredited programs meet standardized quality benchmarks and are recognized by employers nationwide.

## Certification: PTCB vs. ExCPT

Two national certification exams are available for pharmacy technicians. Most employers prefer or require one of these certifications.

### PTCB (Pharmacy Technician Certification Board)

The PTCB Certified Pharmacy Technician (CPhT) credential is the most widely recognized certification in the field. The exam consists of 90 multiple-choice questions (80 scored, 10 unscored) and covers nine knowledge domains:

1. Medications (approximately 40% of the exam)
2. Federal requirements (12.5%)
3. Patient safety and quality assurance (26.25%)
4. Order entry and processing (21.25%)

You have two hours to complete the exam. A passing score is 1,400 out of a possible 1,600 points. The exam fee is $129 as of 2026.

### ExCPT (Exam for the Certification of Pharmacy Technicians)

The ExCPT is offered by the National Healthcareer Association (NHA). It consists of 100 scored questions plus 20 pretest questions, with a two-hour time limit. The content covers similar topics to the PTCB exam but with slightly different weighting.

Both certifications are accepted in most states, but PTCB is generally more recognized by employers. If you are unsure which to pursue, PTCB is the safer choice.

## How to Prepare for the PTCB Exam

### Study Materials

**Official PTCB practice exams** are available on the PTCB website and are one of the best preparation tools. They use the same format and difficulty level as the actual exam.

**Study guides** from publishers like Mosby's, Kaplan, and Barron's provide comprehensive content review and practice questions. Choose one that aligns with the most current exam blueprint.

**Flashcard apps** are excellent for memorizing brand and generic drug names, which make up a significant portion of the exam. Apps like Quizlet have pre-made pharmacy technician flashcard sets that cover the top 200 drugs.

### Study Strategy

Dedicate 8 to 12 weeks for exam preparation if you are studying while working. Focus your study time on the areas weighted most heavily on the exam, particularly medications and patient safety.

Practice pharmaceutical calculations extensively. These questions are straightforward if you know the formulas but can be time-consuming if you are not comfortable with the math. Key calculation topics include:

- Dosage conversions between metric and household units
- Concentration and dilution calculations
- Day supply calculations
- Pediatric dosing based on weight
- IV flow rate calculations

Take at least three full-length practice exams under timed conditions before your test date. Review every question you get wrong and understand why the correct answer is correct.

## State-Specific Requirements

Pharmacy technician regulation varies significantly by state. Some states require certification before you can work, while others allow you to work under a pharmacist's supervision while pursuing certification. Key state-specific requirements to research include:

- **Licensure or registration**: Most states require pharmacy technicians to register with the state board of pharmacy
- **Background checks**: Nearly all states require a criminal background check
- **Continuing education**: Certified technicians must complete continuing education to maintain their certification (typically 20 hours every two years for PTCB)
- **Certification deadlines**: Some states give you a specific timeframe to obtain certification after beginning work

Check your state board of pharmacy's website for the most current requirements.

## Salary and Job Outlook

### What Do Pharmacy Technicians Earn?

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the median annual salary for pharmacy technicians in 2025 was approximately $38,350, or about $18.44 per hour. However, pay varies considerably based on setting and location:

- **Retail pharmacy** (CVS, Walgreens, Walmart): $15.00 to $20.00 per hour
- **Hospital pharmacy**: $17.00 to $24.00 per hour
- **Mail-order pharmacy**: $16.00 to $21.00 per hour
- **Specialty pharmacy**: $18.00 to $26.00 per hour
- **Nuclear pharmacy**: $22.00 to $30.00 per hour (requires additional training)

Geographic location also affects pay. Pharmacy technicians in states like California, Washington, and Alaska earn significantly more than those in southern and midwestern states, though cost of living differences account for much of this gap.

### Job Outlook

The demand for pharmacy technicians is projected to grow 6 percent from 2022 to 2032, which is faster than the average for all occupations. Several factors drive this growth, including an aging population requiring more medications, expanded pharmacist roles that create more delegation to technicians, and increased use of pharmacy services for vaccinations and health screenings.

## Career Advancement Options

Pharmacy technician is not necessarily a dead-end position. Several advancement paths are available:

### Senior or Lead Technician

Experienced technicians can move into lead or senior roles that involve training new staff, managing inventory, or coordinating workflow. These positions typically come with higher pay and more responsibility.

### Specialized Certifications

The PTCB offers advanced certifications in specific areas:

- **Compounded Sterile Preparation Technician (CSPT)**: For technicians working in sterile compounding environments
- **Assessment-Based Certificate Programs**: In areas like medication history, hazardous drug management, and technician product verification

### Transition to Pharmacist

Some pharmacy technicians use their experience as a foundation for pursuing a Doctor of Pharmacy (PharmD) degree. Working as a technician provides valuable exposure to pharmacy practice and can strengthen your pharmacy school application.

### Related Healthcare Careers

Experience as a pharmacy technician can also lead to transitions into pharmaceutical sales, healthcare administration, medical coding, or clinical research coordination.

## Final Thoughts

Becoming a certified pharmacy technician is one of the most accessible entry points into the healthcare field. The training timeline is relatively short, the certification process is straightforward with adequate preparation, and the career offers genuine stability and growth potential.

If you are drawn to healthcare, enjoy detail-oriented work, and want a career that makes a tangible difference in people's lives, pharmacy technology is worth serious consideration. Start by researching accredited programs in your area and check your state's specific requirements. The path from where you are now to a certified pharmacy technician can be as short as six months to a year.

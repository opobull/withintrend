Screen Time That’s Actually Worth It#

Every parent faces the screen time dilemma. You know too much passive screen time isn’t great for kids, but you also know that the right educational apps can genuinely accelerate learning. The trick is finding apps that are engaging enough that kids actually want to use them — because the best educational app in the world is useless if your child closes it after 30 seconds.

After extensive testing and research, here are the apps that successfully thread that needle: genuinely educational and genuinely enjoyable.

Best for Ages 3-6: Building Foundations#

Khan Academy Kids (Free)#

This is the gold standard for early childhood education apps. Khan Academy Kids covers reading, math, social-emotional learning, and creative expression through interactive activities, stories, and games. The content is developed by education experts and aligned with learning standards.

What kids love: The animated characters that guide them through activities, the reward system that keeps them motivated, and the variety of activity types that prevent boredom.

What parents love: It’s completely free with no ads and no in-app purchases. Seriously — there’s no catch. It’s funded by donations to Khan Academy.

Ages: 2-8 | Subjects: Reading, math, social skills | Cost: Free

Endless Alphabet (One-time purchase ~$9)#

Kids drag animated letters into place to spell words, then watch hilarious animations that illustrate each word’s meaning. It’s simple, addictive, and surprisingly effective at building vocabulary and letter recognition.

What kids love: The goofy monster animations are genuinely funny. Kids often watch the definitions multiple times voluntarily.

What parents love: No subscriptions, no ads. One purchase covers all content.

Ages: 3-6 | Subjects: Vocabulary, letters | Cost: ~$9 one-time

Moose Math by Duck Duck Moose (Free)#

A math adventure game where kids help a moose run a juice stand, pet shop, and other businesses by solving math problems. Covers counting, addition, subtraction, sorting, and geometry through play.

What kids love: The cute characters and the sense of running their own business.

What parents love: It adapts to the child’s level automatically and covers multiple math concepts.

Ages: 3-7 | Subjects: Math | Cost: Free

Best for Ages 6-10: Growing Skills#

Prodigy Math (Free with premium option)#

Prodigy disguises math practice as an RPG (role-playing game). Kids create a character, explore a fantasy world, and battle monsters by solving math problems. The math curriculum covers grades 1-8 and adapts to each child’s level.

What kids love: The game is legitimately fun. Many kids don’t realize how much math they’re practicing because they’re focused on leveling up their character and collecting pets.

What parents love: Detailed reports showing which skills your child is mastering and where they need help. The free version covers all math content.

Ages: 6-14 | Subjects: Math | Cost: Free (premium adds cosmetic features)

Epic! (Subscription ~$10/month)#

A digital library with over 40,000 children’s books, audiobooks, and educational videos. Think Netflix but for kids’ books. The recommendation engine learns what your child likes and suggests similar titles.

What kids love: Unlimited access to books on any topic they’re interested in, from dinosaurs to space to graphic novels.

What parents love: Reading reports, the ability to set time limits, and the fact that your child is actually reading instead of watching random YouTube videos.

Ages: 4-12 | Subjects: Reading | Cost: ~$10/month (free for educators)

ScratchJr and Scratch (Free)#

MIT’s coding platforms for kids are brilliant. ScratchJr (ages 5-7) uses visual blocks to create animations and simple games. Scratch (ages 8-16) is more sophisticated, allowing kids to build real interactive projects with visual programming.

What kids love: Creating their own games and animations. The sense of accomplishment when something they built actually works is powerful.

What parents love: It’s genuinely teaching computational thinking and problem-solving. Many professional programmers started with Scratch.

Ages: 5-16 | Subjects: Coding, logic | Cost: Free

National Geographic Kids (Free with some premium)#

Explore the natural world through articles, videos, quizzes, and games from one of the most trusted science brands. Content covers animals, science, geography, history, and space.

What kids love: The incredible photography and videos of animals and natural phenomena. The quizzes are engaging without feeling like tests.

What parents love: Trusted, accurate science content that sparks genuine curiosity.

Ages: 6-14 | Subjects: Science, geography | Cost: Free with premium content

Best for Ages 10+: Advanced Learning#

Duolingo (Free with premium option)#

The world’s most popular language-learning app works for kids and adults alike. The gamified approach — streaks, XP, leaderboards, and a persistent owl mascot — keeps kids motivated to practice daily.

What kids love: The competitive element (beating friends’ streaks and scores) and the bite-sized lessons that feel manageable.

What parents love: It actually works for building language foundations. Over 40 languages available.

Ages: 10+ | Subjects: Languages | Cost: Free (premium removes ads)

Brilliant (Subscription ~$13/month)#

Brilliant takes a completely different approach to learning: interactive problem-solving instead of passive video watching. Courses in math, science, computer science, and data analysis use visual, hands-on exercises that build genuine understanding.

What kids love: The puzzles feel like games, and the visual explanations make complex concepts click.

What parents love: It develops real problem-solving skills, not just memorization. Content goes from middle school through college level.

Ages: 10+ | Subjects: Math, science, CS | Cost: ~$13/month

Codecademy (Free basic plan)#

For older kids interested in programming, Codecademy provides structured courses in Python, JavaScript, HTML/CSS, and other languages. Kids write real code from lesson one.

What kids love: Building actual websites and programs that they can show friends.

What parents love: Learning to code is one of the most valuable skills a young person can develop.

Ages: 12+ | Subjects: Programming | Cost: Free basic, Pro ~$20/month

The Creativity Builders#

GarageBand (Free on Apple devices)#

Apple’s music creation app is surprisingly powerful and accessible. Kids can play virtual instruments, record songs, mix tracks, and learn music theory through doing.

Ages: 8+ | Subjects: Music | Cost: Free

Tinkercad (Free)#

A free 3D modeling tool that’s simple enough for kids but capable enough for real projects. Kids can design objects, see them in 3D, and even 3D print their creations.

Ages: 8+ | Subjects: 3D design, engineering | Cost: Free

Stop Motion Studio (Free with premium)#

Kids create stop-motion animated films using figures, clay, or anything else. It teaches patience, storytelling, and the basics of animation.

Ages: 6+ | Subjects: Art, storytelling | Cost: Free (Pro ~$5)

How to Choose the Right Apps#

Match the child’s interests. A dinosaur-loving kid will engage more with a science app. A story-loving kid will thrive with Epic! Force-fitting doesn’t work.

Check for adaptive learning. The best apps adjust difficulty based on the child’s performance. Too easy leads to boredom. Too hard leads to frustration.

Avoid apps that are secretly just ads. If an app constantly pushes in-app purchases or shows ads between activities, find an alternative. Good educational apps prioritize learning over monetization.

Set time limits. Even great educational apps shouldn’t replace physical play, social interaction, and unstructured creative time. Use screen time management features built into iOS and Android.

Sit with them sometimes. Educational apps work best when parents occasionally participate. Ask what they’re learning, celebrate progress, and show genuine interest.

The Bottom Line#

The right educational apps don’t replace good teachers, engaged parents, or real-world experiences. But they can supplement all three in powerful ways. When a child genuinely enjoys learning something through an app — and carries that curiosity into the rest of their day — that’s screen time well spent.

Start with one or two apps from this list, see what clicks, and build from there. The goal isn’t to fill every minute with educational content — it’s to make the screen time they do have count.
